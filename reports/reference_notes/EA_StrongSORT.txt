ABSTRACT
MRI-guided radiotherapy (MRIgRT) enables real-time visualization of tumor motion, allowing adaptive radiation delivery based
on dynamic anatomical changes. However, respiratory-induced tumor motion remains a major challenge, particularly for
thoracic and abdominal tumors. Continuous tumor motion may reduce treatment accuracy and increase radiation exposure
to surrounding healthy tissues. Therefore, reliable and efficient tumor tracking is essential for real-time motion management
in MRI-guided radiotherapy. Recent advances in artificial intelligence have demonstrated significant potential for medical
image analysis; however, many existing tumor tracking approaches rely on segmentation-based methods that require detailed
annotations and complex processing, which can limit their use in real-time clinical environments. In this work, we propose a
detection-based tumor tracking framework that integrates the YOLOv11 object detection model with an enhanced StrongSORT
tracking algorithm (EA-StrongSORT). The proposed approach replaces the conventional re-identification backbone with a
lightweight EfficientNetV2 architecture augmented with an Efficient Channel Attention (ECA) mechanism. The overall framework
follows a tracking-by-detection concept, where tumor regions are first detected and subsequently associated across frames.
The proposed framework is evaluated on the TrackRAD2025 dataset using multiple YOLOv11 variants to analyze the balance
between performance and model complexity. Experimental results demonstrate that lightweight YOLOv11n model achieve
the best detection performance, with a precision of 0.914, mAP of 0.778, and mAP50−95 of 0.607. Furthermore, the proposed
tracking framework achieves stable temporal association, with MOTA scores exceeding 91% and HOTA scores around 90%.
The proposed framework provides an efficient and reliable solution for real-time tumor localization in cine-MRI.
Introduction
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) has become a fundamental component of modern radiation oncology due to its superior
soft-tissue contrast and its ability to visualize tumors and surrounding organs without the use of ionizing radiation. Recent
advances in MRI-guided radiotherapy, particularly hybrid MRI–linear accelerator systems, have enabled continuous image
acquisition during treatment delivery and real-time monitoring. This capability offers significant clinical advantages, including
improved target visualization, adaptive treatment strategies, and enhanced motion awareness. However, it also introduces
challenges associated with dynamic anatomical changes during imaging and treatment delivery1, 2.
Among the most critical sources of uncertainty in MRI-guided radiotherapy workflows is respiratory-induced motion. As
patients breathe freely during scanning and treatment, tumors particularly in thoracic and upper-abdominal regions—undergo
continuous displacement and deformation. If not adequately managed, this motion can result in misalignment between the
planned and delivered radiation dose, potentially compromising tumor coverage. Therefore, accurate and continuous tumor
localization is essential for safe and effective treatment3. In this context, tumor tracking in cine-MRI has emerged as a key
enabling technology for real-time imaging and adaptive radiotherapy. Cine-MRI sequences capture dynamic anatomical

changes over time, providing a direct representation of respiratory motion and tumor movement. However, reliable tracking

per-frame processing to support applications such as beam gating and online treatment adaptation4.
From a computational perspective, tumor tracking in cine-MRI extends beyond static localization and requires maintaining
spatial and temporal consistency across sequential frames. This involves not only detecting the tumor in each frame but also
preserving its identity over time despite respiratory motion, appearance variations, and imaging artifacts. Cine-MRI sequences
may span hundreds to thousands of frames across multiple respiratory cycles, making temporal consistency a critical requirement
for clinically viable tracking systems 5. Prior work on cine-MRI tumor tracking and MRI-guided radiotherapy highlights
that clinically applicable solutions must ensure robustness, scalability, and computational efficiency while maintaining stable
localization over long temporal sequences6. While segmentation-based and patient-specific deep learning approaches have
demonstrated strong spatial accuracy, they often rely on dense annotations, patient-specific adaptation, or computationally
expensive optimization procedures, which can limit their scalability and real-time applicability7, 8. These limitations motivate
the exploration of detection-based tracking paradigms that enable efficient and continuous tumor localization without requiring
pixel-wise annotations.
Within this context, tumor tracking performance in this study is framed in terms of continuous localization accuracy and
temporal association quality across sequential frames, rather than isolated frame-level detection results. This perspective aligns
with clinical requirements, where stable and interpretable tracking outputs are essential for downstream applications such as
motion monitoring, gating, and adaptive radiotherapy9, 10. By grounding the evaluation within the TrackRAD2025 benchmark5,
this work aims to assess the effectiveness of detection-based tracking pipelines as a practical alternative to segmentation-driven

identification backbone with a lightweight EfficientNetV211 based architecture augmented with an Efficient Channel Attention
(ECA) mechanism, improving feature representation while maintaining computational efficiency. The overall system follows a
tracking-by-detection paradigm, where tumor regions are detected independently in each frame and subsequently associated
across time. The framework is evaluated on the TrackRAD2025 dataset5 (i.e. Figure (1) shows example cine-MRI frames from

• We introduce an improved tracking module, referred to as Efficient Attention StrongSORT (EA-StrongSORT). by replac-

Related work
Recent advances in tumor tracking for MRI-guided radiotherapy have increasingly relied on artificial intelligence to address
challenges such as respiratory motion, appearance variability, and real-time constraints. Accurate and stable tumor localization
during cine-MRI acquisition is essential for effective motion management during treatment delivery. As a result, a wide range
of algorithmic approaches has been developed, differing in how they address spatial localization, temporal consistency, and
computational efficiency. These approaches can be broadly categorized into segmentation-based, memory-augmented, and
motion estimation methods. One emerging direction in tumor tracking explores the use of foundation vision models. The work
in7 investigates fine-tuning the Segment Anything Model (SAM) on the TrackRAD2025 dataset using sparse prompts such as
bounding boxes or masks in the first frame, followed by frame-by-frame propagation. This approach achieves competitive
performance, with Dice similarity scores approaching 0.88 while maintaining near real-time inference. However, such methods
rely on prompt intialization and do not explicitly model object identity across frames, limiting their robustness for long- term
and fully automated tracking, particularly under rapid tumor deformation or appearance variation. To explicitly address temporal
consistency, memory-augmented segmentation-based tracking approaches have been proposed. In particular, XMem-based

remain prone to error accumulation over long sequences, limiting their reliability in real-time clinical settings.
Another line of work focuses on motion-resolved tumor segmentation as an indirect means of supporting tracking. The

their suitability for real-time tracking applications. Segmentation-based approaches using deep neural networks have also been

volumes across respiratory phases, achieving competitive segmentation accuracy8. In addition, AI-based systems have been
developed for real-time tumor segmentation and tool tracking in interventional imaging scenarios16 . However, these approaches
are often computationally expensive and rely on dense annotations, which limits their applicability in real-time cine-MRI
tracking under continuous respiratory motion. Taken together, prior research highlights a growing shift toward data-driven
and segmentation-based tumor tracking approaches, with promising results in accuracy and robustness. However, several
challenges remain, particularly in achieving full automation, maintaining temporal consistency over long cine-MRI sequences,
and ensuring computational efficiency under real–time constraints. Most existing methods rely heavily on segmentation-
based paradigms and often lack robust mechanisms for long-term temporal association. In contrast, detection-based tracking
formulations, such as those enabled by datasets like TrackRAD2025, offer an alternative perspective by framing tumor tracking
as a detection-and-association problem. This motivates the exploration of lightweight detection-based tracking pipelines that
can achieve stable and continuous tumor localization while maintaining real-time performance. In this context, the present work
investigates a detection-based tracking framework that integrates efficient object detection with enhanced temporal association
mechanism.

Proposed Framework
In this section, we introduce the proposed tumor detection and tracking framework from cine-MRI sequences. The proposed
framework employs YOLOv11 as the detection backbone for tumor localization, followed by an enhanced StrongSORT-based
tracking module to ensure temporal consistency across frames. Unlike conventional StrongSORT pipelines that typically rely on
YOLOX for object detection, YOLOv11 is adopted in this work to provide improved detection performance. To ensure robust
temporal tracking, the detection module is combined with an enhanced StrongSORT pipeline named as Efficient Attention
StrongSort (EA-StrongSort). The main contribution of the model is replacing the original ReID feature extractor StrongSORT
with a lightweight EfficeintNEtV2 based backbone augmented with an Efficient Channel Attention (ECA) mechanism. This
modification improves feature representation while maintaining low computational complexity. Figure 2 presents the overall
framework and in the following section each phase will be described in detail.
Tumor Detection using YOLOv11
YOLOv11 is a state-of-the-art real-time object detection framework designed to balance high detection accuracy with com-
putational efficiency17. YOLOv11 employs a single-stage decision paradigm similar to prior YOLO models, where object
localization and classification are performed simultaneously within a unified neural network. This architecture eliminates the
need for separate region proposal stages, facilitating efficient real-time performance. The model follows the typical YOLO
design, compromising three primary components: the backbone, neck, and detection head.
The backbone extracts hierarchical feature representations from the input images via a sequence of convolutional layers
and feature extraction blocks, capturing both low-level visual patterns and high-level semantic information. The neck module
subsequently aggregates and refines the extracted features to improve multi-scale object detection, typically integrating feature
fusion mechanisms such as Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) and Path Aggregation Networks (PAN). These enhance the
model’s ability to detect objects of different sizes by fusing information from multiple feature levels. Finally, the detection head
produces the final predictions, including bounding box coordinates, object confidence scores, and class probabilities across
multiple scales. This multi-scale approach improves the accuracy of detecting small, medium, and large objects within the
same image. Through targeted architectural optimizations and enhanced feature representation, YOLOv11 achieves a balanced
trade-off between computational efficiency, detection accuracy, and inference speed, making it suitable for a wide range of
real-time computer vision applications.
YOLOv11 provides multiple model variants, as shown in Table 1, including Nano, Small, Medium, and Large, to balance
computational complexity, inference speed, and detection accuracy depending on the application requirements18. The YOLOv11-
Nano is the most lightweight variant, optimized for resource-constrained environments and real-time applications, delivering
fast inference with minimal computational overhead. The YOLOv11-Small model increases network capacity over the Nano

Proposed Multi-object Tracking using EA-StrongSORT
After detecting tumor regions using the YOLOv11 detector, a tracking module is required to maintain the temporal consistency
of the detected objects across consecutive frames. Object detection processes each frame independently and therefore cannot
guarantee that the same object will preserve its identity across time. For this reason, a multi-object tracking module is integrated
after the detection stage to associate detections across frames and maintain consistent identities.
In order to improve the appearance modeling capability of the tracking framework, a modified StrongSORT architecture
is proposed in this work, a tracking framework based on the StrongSORT algorithm is adopted due to its effectiveness in
multi-object tracking tasks 19. StrongSORT extends the DeepSORT tracking framework by improving appearance feature
representation and association mechanisms, which allows more reliable tracking in challenging scenarios such as occlusion,
motion variation, and appearance similarities20.
The proposed model, known as Efficient Attention StrongSORT (EA-StrongSORT), introduces a lightweight but effective
feature extraction backbone designed to enhance tracking robustness while maintaining computational efficiency. In the original
StrongSORT architecture21, appearance features are extracted using a ResNet50 backbone. Although ResNet50 provides strong
representation capability, it introduces relatively high computational complexity and may not efficiently capture the subtle
visual differences between detected tumor regions in medical imaging22. To address this limitation, as shown in Figure 2, the
proposed EA-StrongSORT replaces the original backbone with a modified EfficientNetV2-based architecture to improve feature
extraction efficiency while maintaining strong representation capability.
Figure. 3 presents the modified EfficientNetV2-based architecture where the pipeline begins with an initial convolution
layer that captures low-level spatial information from the detected object regions. This layer is followed by a sequence of
Fused-MBConv and MBConv blocks, which progressively extract hierarchical feature representations. The Fused-MBConv
blocks are particularly effective in early stages of the network, where standard depthwise convolutions may slow down training.
These blocks combine convolution and expansion operations into a single structure, allowing faster feature extraction with
reduced computational overhead. Subsequently, deeper MBConv layers are used to capture higher-level semantic features
that represent more complex visual patterns of tumor regions. These layers enable the network to generate discriminative
representations that are essential for distinguishing between visually similar regions. Furthermore, the number of blocks at each
stage is reduced compared to the original EfficientNetV2 ensuring lower computational costs while preserving essential feature

To further enhance the discriminative capability of the appearance features, the feature extraction network is extended
with an attention mechanism. In particular, an Efficient Channel Attention (ECA)23 module is integrated within the MBConv
blocks in order to emphasize informative feature channels while suppressing irrelevant information. The ECA enhances feature
representation by assigning adaptive importance weights to each channel. First, global average pooling ( GAP) is applied
to the input feature map ( X), followed by one-dimensional convolution and sigmoid activation channel weights defined as
w =σ(Conv1D(GAP(X))) . The refined feature map X′ = X ⊙ wis then obtained by channel-wise multiplication, emphasizing
informative features while suppressing irrelevant features.
As shown in 4, the modified MBConv block consists of a sequence of convolutional operations followed by a lightweight
channel attention mechanism. First, depthwise convolution is applied to capture spatial feature representations from the detected
object region. The extracted feature maps are then passed through the ECA module, which adaptively assigns importance
weights to different feature channels. Unlike traditional attention mechanisms that rely on dimensionality reduction, the ECA
module captures channel dependencies using a lightweight one-dimensional convolution. This design enables the model to
improve feature representation without introducing significant computational overhead. After the attention weights are applied,
a pointwise convolution (1×1 convolution) is used to fuse the enhanced feature channels and generate the final representation.
Attention mechanisms are not uniformly beneficial across all network stages 24. Applying attention in early layers may
introduce unnecessary complexity and noise, as these layers primarily capture low-level patterns. The reduction in the model
compleixty makes the framework suitable for real-time clinical deployment. Therefore, in the proposed model, ECA is applied
only in deeper layers where feature representations are more beneficial for tumor discrimination. The design choice supports the
prevention of over-smoothing of features and reduces the risk of overfitting. These refined features are then used to construct
appearance embeddings for each detected object. By incorporating channel attention into the feature extraction process, the
proposed EA-StrongSORT framework becomes more capable of highlighting subtle visual patterns that distinguish different
objects. This improvement leads to more discriminative appearance embeddings, which ultimately enhances the robustness of
object association during tracking. The appearance embeddingf i =φ( xi) is extracted from the detected object region xi) using
the feature extraction function φ(·), which presents the proposed EfficientNetV211 based backbone integrated with the ECA

Material and Dataset
Experiments are conducted using the TrackRAD2025 dataset5, a large-scale benchmark specifically designed for tumor tracking
in cine-MRI sequences acquired during MRI-guided radiotherapy. The dataset comprises temporally dense image sequences that
capture natural respiratory motion across multiple anatomical sites and imaging systems. It provides frame-level bounding-box
annotations for tumor targets rather than pixel-wise segmentation masks, thereby naturally formulating tumor tracking as
a tracking-by-detection problem with explicit temporal linking. This annotation design enables the evaluation of tracking
pipelines that prioritize temporal continuity and identity preservation over detailed boundary delineation, which is particularly
relevant for real-time clinical applications. The dataset further reflects substantial clinical variability, including differences in
tumor size, anatomical location, imaging protocols, and motion patterns across patients.
It includes thoracic, abdominal, and pelvic tumors acquired on different MRI-linac systems, with motion amplitudes
varying according to anatomical site and breathing pattern. This diversity introduces challenges related to generalization and
robustness, reinforcing the importance of tracking strategies that can maintain temporal consistency across heterogeneous
imaging conditions. The dataset comprises 585 patients collected from 6 international centers, including thoracic, abdominal,
and pelvic tumors acquired using both 0.35 T and 1.5 T MRI-linac systems. It provides over 2.8 million unlabeled cine-MRI
frames from 477 patients and more than 10,000 annotated frames from 108 patients, with sequence lengths ranging from 20 to
20,543 frames per scan. The dataset is split into 527 training cases (477 unlabeled, 50 labeled) and 58 fully labeled test cases,
enabling large-scale evaluation for real-time tumor tracking under realistic clinical variability.

Results
The detection performance of YOLOv11 framework was quantitatively evaluated using different model scales to establish
baseline performance prior to applying fold-based training and tracking strategies. Four YOLOv11 variants were considered in
this analysis, namely YOLOv11n, YOLOv11m, YOLOv11l, and YOLOv11xl. The evaluation metrics included precision, recall,
mean Average Precision (mAP), and mAP@50-95, as summarized in Table 2. The YOLOv11n variant achieves the highest
precision value (0.914), indicating a strong ability to correctly detect tumor regions while minimizing false positive predictions.
In comparison, larger model variants exhibit a gradual reduction in recall, with YOLOv11xl achieving the lowest recall value
(0.386). This behavior suggests that increasing model complexity does not necessarily translate into improved sensitivity for the
given dataset. A similar trend is observed in the mAP results. YOLOv11n attains the highest overall mAP (0.778), followed by
YOLOv11m (0.724), whereas YOLOv11l and YOLOv11xl demonstrate lower performance. The mAP@50-95 metric further
confirms this observation, reflecting reduced robustness across stricter intersection-over-union thresholds for larger model
configurations. Overall, these findings indicate that the smaller YOLOv11 variants provide a more effective balance between
detection accuracy and generalization capability in this application. Based on these quantitative results, YOLOv11n is selected
as the baseline detection model for subsequent experiments involving fold-based training and tracking integration.
To further evaluate the robustness and generalization capability of the detection model, a fold-based training strategy was
employed using the selected YOLOv11n configuration. The model was trained and evaluated across five different data folds

Similarly, recall values show limited fluctuation, suggesting that the model maintains a comparable sensitivity to tumor detection
regardless of the specific training-testing split. The mAP and mAP@50-95 metrics further confirm the robustness of the
fold-based approach. Although minor performance variations are observed across folds, the overall detection accuracy remains
consistently high, reflecting strong generalization capability. These findings indicate that the observed detection performance is

Discussion
This study presents a comprehensive evaluation of a YOLOv11-based detection framework integrated with fold-based training
and a modified StrongSORT tracking module for tumor detection and tracking. The discussion interprets the results obtained
and highlights the key observations derived from both quantitative and qualitative results.
The quantitative detection results demonstrate that model scale plays a critical role in overall detection performance.
Among the evaluated YOLOv11 variants, the smaller model configuration achieves superior performance in terms of precision,
recall, and mAP metrics. This observation suggests that increasing model complexity does not necessarily improve the
detection accuracy of the data set studied. Given the relatively limited size and variability of the cine-MRI data, larger
models such as YOLOv11l and YOLOv11xl are more prone to overfitting and may fail to generalize effectively. In addition,

Conclusion
This study presented a detection-based tumor tracking framework for cine_MRI sequences by integrating YOLOv11 with
a modified EA-StrongSORT tracking module. The proposed approach replaces the original StrongSORT backbone with a
lightweight EfficientNetV2-based architecture and introduces a selective attention mechanism to enhance feature representation.
Unlike conventional designs, attention is applied only in the deeper network layers, improving discriminative capability
while avoiding additional computational overhead. The experimental results on the TrackRAD2025 dataset demonstrate that
lightweight detection models, particularly YOLOv11n, provide a strong balance between accuracy and generalization. The
integration of the proposed tracking module further improves the temporal consistency, achieving stable and reliable tumor
localization across frames. The proposed framework highlights the effectiveness of combining the lightweight architecture
with a selective attention mechanism for real-time tracking tasks. This design achieves improved efficiency and robustness,
making it suitable for clinical implementation where both accuracy and computational costs are essential. Future work will
focus on validating the proposed approach on larger multi-center datasets, exploring advanced temporal modeling strategies,
and extending the framework to more complex multi-class tracking frameworks.
